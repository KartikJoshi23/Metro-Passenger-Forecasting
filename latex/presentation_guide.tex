\section{Part 1 --- Problem \& Data \textnormal{(Kartik)}}
\textbf{Say:} ``Our project predicts how many people will enter each Dubai Metro station in the
\emph{next hour}, so the operator can add trains and staff \emph{before} a crowd forms instead of
reacting after.''

\textbf{Show:} the Live tab headline number and the demand curve.

\textbf{Key points to land:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The problem.} Too few trains $\rightarrow$ dangerous crowding and long waits; too
        many $\rightarrow$ wasted energy and money. Good forecasts hit the right amount at the right time.
  \item \textbf{Real data.} We use Dubai's official open data (RTA, via the Dubai Pulse portal):
        real smart-card tap records. After cleaning we have \textbf{$\approx$503{,}000 entries},
        \textbf{42 days}, \textbf{53 stations} --- all from \textbf{2026} (the latest data).
  \item \textbf{Proof it is real.} The busiest stations our data finds --- BurJuman, Union,
        Al~Rigga, Mall of the Emirates, Burj~Khalifa/Dubai~Mall --- are exactly Dubai Metro's known
        busy stations. Red line carries $\approx$70\% of taps.
  \item \textbf{Why only 2026?} Older years had very few days at much lower ridership; mixing them
        would teach the model out-of-date patterns. Newest data = most useful.
\end{itemize}

\section{Concepts cheat-sheet (super simple)}
\term{Forecasting} guessing what comes next from what already happened (like guessing tomorrow's
weather from this week).

\term{Passenger inflow} how many people \emph{tap in} (enter) at a station in a time slot.

\term{Time series} a list of numbers in time order (people each hour, hour after hour).

\term{Neural network} a computer model that learns patterns from many examples instead of being
given fixed rules.

\term{RNN (Recurrent Neural Network)} a neural network \emph{with a memory}. It reads a sequence one
step at a time and remembers what it just saw --- like reading a sentence word by word and keeping
the earlier words in mind. Great for time series.

\term{LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)} a smarter RNN. It has tiny ``gates'' that decide what to
\emph{keep}, what to \emph{forget}, and what to \emph{output}, so it can remember important things
for a long time and ignore noise --- like a student with a good notebook who keeps key facts and
drops the rest.

\term{GRU (Gated Recurrent Unit)} a lighter, faster cousin of the LSTM with fewer gates --- similar
idea, slightly simpler.

\term{CNN (Convolutional Neural Network)} usually used for images; it slides a small window to spot
\emph{local} patterns. In \textbf{CNN-LSTM}, the CNN spots short bursts and the LSTM tracks them
over time.

\term{Our hybrid (LSTM $\Vert$ RNN)} we run an LSTM \emph{and} a SimpleRNN on the same input
side-by-side and join their answers --- the LSTM brings longer memory, the RNN a quick read of
recent momentum.

\term{Ensemble} ask several models and average their answers --- like asking several friends and
taking the average guess. Averaging cancels out random luck, so results are steadier and a bit
better. Our solution averages three trained copies.

\term{Station embedding} a small set of numbers the model \emph{learns} as an ``ID card'' for each
station, so it knows BurJuman behaves differently from a quiet stop.

\term{Climatology} the ``usual'' pattern --- the historical average for that station, hour and
weekday (e.g.\ ``8\,am is usually busy''). We feed it as a hint, and also use it as a baseline.

\term{Calibration} a final nudge so predictions are not always a little too low or too high ---
like fixing a ruler that always reads 1\,cm short.

\term{Features} the clues we give the model: the hour, day-of-week, weekend flag, recent counts,
the station ID and the ``usual'' value.

\term{Training / Validation / Test} study notes / practice quiz / final exam. We measure accuracy on
the \emph{test} data the model never saw, so the score is honest.

\term{Data leakage} accidentally letting the model peek at exam answers while studying. We avoided
it: the ``usual'' values and calibration are computed only from training/validation days, and we
split by date (past to predict the future), never randomly.

\section{Every algorithm in the comparison (what \& why)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Naive (persistence)} --- the simplest possible guess: ``next hour = this hour.''
        It is the bar every real model must beat.
  \item \textbf{Climatology} --- ``next hour = the usual value for this hour/day.'' Captures the
        typical shape but ignores how busy \emph{today} actually is, so it is weak on its own.
  \item \textbf{RNN (only)} --- a plain recurrent network reading the last 6 hours.
  \item \textbf{LSTM (only)} --- a stacked LSTM; better long memory than a plain RNN.
  \item \textbf{GRU} --- a lighter gated network; fast, competitive.
  \item \textbf{CNN-LSTM} --- a CNN finds short patterns, an LSTM tracks them over time.
  \item \textbf{LSTM\,+\,RNN (ours)} --- the parallel hybrid above, deployed as a 3-model ensemble
        with bias calibration. Lowest error overall and the model the dashboard uses.
\end{itemize}
All learning models share the \emph{same} inputs (station ID, hour, weekend, recent counts, the
``usual'' value); only the sequence core differs --- so the comparison is fair.

\section{The accuracy numbers (MAE, RMSE, MAPE, R$^2$)}
Think of an ``error'' as: \emph{predicted minus actual} passengers for one hour at one station.

\term{MAE (Mean Absolute Error)} the average size of the error, ignoring sign. ``On average we are
off by about 4--5 passengers per station-hour.'' \textbf{Lower is better.}

\term{RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error)} similar, but it \emph{punishes big misses more} (it squares
the errors first). If RMSE is much bigger than MAE, a few large mistakes exist. \textbf{Lower is better.}

\term{MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error)} the error as a \emph{percentage} of the real value.
``About 30\% off on average.'' Good for comparing busy and quiet stations fairly. \textbf{Lower is better.}

\term{R$^2$ (R-squared)} how much of the up-and-down pattern the model explains, from 0 to 1.
$1.0$ = perfect; $0$ = no better than always guessing the average; \emph{negative} = worse than that.
Our \textbf{0.87} means we explain about \textbf{87\%} of the variation. \textbf{Higher is better.}

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Quick line if asked ``why is your MAE close to others but you still win?'':} our
\textbf{MAPE} is clearly better, meaning we are much more accurate at the quieter stations and
off-peak hours where ``just repeat the last hour'' fails.

\section{Probable questions --- with answers}
Grouped from \textbf{easy} to \textbf{advanced}. Answers are short on purpose --- say them in your
own words.

\subsection{Easy (definitions \& basics)}
\q{In one line, what does your project do?}
\an{It predicts how many passengers will enter each Dubai Metro station in the next hour, live.}

\q{What is forecasting?}
\an{Using past data to estimate a future value --- here, next hour's passenger entries.}

\q{Is your data real?}
\an{Yes --- official RTA smart-card tap records from Dubai's open-data portal (Dubai Pulse), 2026.}

\q{What is an RNN?}
\an{A neural network with memory that reads a sequence step by step and remembers recent steps ---
ideal for time-ordered data.}

\q{What is an LSTM?}
\an{A special RNN with ``gates'' that choose what to remember, forget and output, so it keeps
important information for longer and handles long patterns better than a plain RNN.}

\q{What do MAE and R$^2$ mean?}
\an{MAE = average error size (lower is better, ours $\approx$4.5 passengers). R$^2$ = fraction of
the pattern explained (higher is better, ours $\approx$0.87, i.e.\ 87\%).}

\q{Why Dubai?}
\an{It runs a modern automated metro that produces rich tap data, it is event- and tourism-heavy
with big demand surges, and crowd-forecasting fits its smart-city goals.}

\subsection{Medium (design choices)}
\q{Why ``next hour'' and not next day?}
\an{Next-hour is the operational window --- it is what lets you add a train or open gates in time.
It is the most useful and demonstrable horizon.}

\q{Why combine LSTM and RNN instead of using one?}
\an{The LSTM brings longer memory and the SimpleRNN a compact read of recent momentum; joining them
(and averaging an ensemble) is steadier and scores best in our fair comparison.}

\q{Difference between LSTM and GRU?}
\an{Both are gated RNNs. LSTM has three gates and a separate memory cell; GRU merges them into two
gates --- simpler and faster, often similar accuracy.}

\q{What is the naive baseline and why include it?}
\an{``Next hour = this hour.'' It is the simplest guess; if a model can't beat it, the model is
pointless. Ours beats it on every metric.}

\q{What is climatology and why is it a baseline?}
\an{The historical average for that station/hour/weekday --- the ``usual.'' It captures the typical
shape but not how busy today is, so alone it is weak (it even scores negative R$^2$ on our test).}

\q{Why use only 2026 data?}
\an{$\sim$90\% of the available days are 2026 at today's ridership level; the few old days are much
lower-volume. Mixing them creates a train/test mismatch and teaches outdated patterns.}

\q{What is an ensemble and why does averaging help?}
\an{Training the same model a few times gives slightly different results due to randomness;
averaging them cancels that random noise, lowering error and making the rank stable run-to-run.}

\q{How is the live clock built? What happens at night?}
\an{We compute Dubai time directly (UTC$+$4, no daylight saving). Outside service hours it shows
``metro closed, first trains 05:00''; during service it shows the next-hour forecast. A demo
override lets us simulate any hour for presentations.}

\q{How did you avoid data leakage?}
\an{We split by date (train on earlier days, test on later days), and compute the climatology and
calibration only from training/validation data --- the test days are never used to build features.}

\subsection{Advanced (technical depth)}
\q{Your forecast first looked $\sim$15\% too low. Why, and how did you fix it?}
\an{We trained with the Huber loss, which targets the \emph{median}. Hourly inflow is
right-skewed (lots of small values, occasional big peaks), so the median sits below the mean ---
the model under-predicts. We fixed it with a multiplicative \emph{bias calibration} factor (one for
weekdays, one for weekends) fit only on the validation set, which makes the forecasts unbiased
without touching test data.}

\q{Why is climatology's R$^2$ negative?}
\an{Because each sampled day has a different overall level; a fixed historical average can be
systematically off for a given day, so it does worse than simply predicting the global mean ---
that is what a negative R$^2$ means. The recurrent model fixes this by reading how busy today is.}

\q{Why a chronological split instead of a random one?}
\an{This is a time-series task --- in real life you predict the future from the past. A random split
could let the model ``see'' future days during training, inflating the score. Chronological keeps it honest.}

\q{Why does a one-step next-hour model lag at the very tip of a peak?}
\an{It predicts each hour from the hours just before it; right at a sharp peak those recent hours are
still rising, so a single-step forecast slightly under-shoots the exact peak. Our climatology hint
and ``today's level'' feature reduce it (network tracking $\approx$0.83 on a real day; the typical
curve overlays at $\approx$0.99). Multi-step sequence-to-sequence / attention models predict the
whole horizon at once and reduce this further --- our planned future work.}

\q{What exactly is the station embedding?}
\an{A lookup table that maps each station ID to a small learned vector (8 numbers). The network
learns these during training so stations with similar behaviour get similar vectors --- it lets one
shared model specialise per station without a separate model each.}

\q{Stations affect each other --- why not a Graph Neural Network (DCRNN / STGCN)?}
\an{Spatial graph models (DCRNN, STGCN, Graph WaveNet) capture how a surge propagates between
connected stations and are a strong next step. We deliberately scoped to a robust, explainable
recurrent solution first; the graph extension is in our future-work list.}

\q{What are the limitations of your data and results?}
\an{The open extract is a \emph{sample} of taps, so absolute counts are scaled and a single day's
level varies. We handle it with per-station scaling and calibration, and we are transparent that the
shapes and rankings are real even if raw magnitudes are sampled. Full-population AFC data would
sharpen the absolute numbers.}

\q{How would you take this to production?}
\an{Stream live AFC taps into the same hourly aggregation, run the trained ensemble each hour, and
push next-hour forecasts and crowding alerts to the control room; retrain periodically to track
drift (new lines, events, behaviour change).}

\q{Why parallel LSTM $\Vert$ RNN rather than stacking them?}
\an{Stacking a SimpleRNN after an LSTM sometimes discards useful detail and was unstable in our
tests. Running them in parallel and concatenating keeps both representations and reliably matches or
beats either alone, especially as an averaged ensemble.}

\q{Which metric matters most here, and why?}
\an{For safety/operations we care about large misses at busy times, so \textbf{RMSE} (which punishes
big errors) and \textbf{MAPE} (fairness across station sizes) matter most. Our model leads on both,
which is the real argument --- not just a tiny MAE edge.}
